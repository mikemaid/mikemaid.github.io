Data Curation

Overview of Datasets

For this analysis, we utilized two primary datasets:

  1. Historical Stock Data:
    • Includes daily stock price data (Open, High, Low, Close, and Date) for the Nasdaq 100 over several years.
    • Key attributes:
      • Date: The trading date.
      • Close/Last: The stocks closing price for the day.
      • Open, High, Low: The opening, highest, and lowest prices recorded for the day.
  2. Nasdaq 100 Company Data:
    • Lists the companies included in the Nasdaq 100, along with their tickers, sectors, and sub-industries.
    • Key attributes:
      • Company: The companys name.
      • Ticker: The stock ticker symbol.
      • GICS Sector: The companys sector, categorized using the Global Industry Classification Standard.
      • GICS Sub-Industry: A more granular classification within the sector.

Preprocessing Steps

To ensure the data was ready for analysis, the following preprocessing steps were conducted:

  1. Loading and Inspecting Data:
    • Imported the datasets using pandas.
    • Checked for missing values and data types using isnull() and info() methods.
    • Verified the structure of the datasets by examining the first few rows (head()) and summary statistics (describe()).
  2. Date Parsing and Formatting:
    • Converted the Date column in the Historical Stock Data to a datetime format using pd.to_datetime() for easy manipulation and analysis (e.g., grouping by year).
  3. Handling Missing Data:
    • No missing values were found in either dataset. If missing values were present, strategies such as forward-filling or interpolation would have been applied, depending on the column.
  4. Renaming Columns:
    • Renamed columns (e.g., replacing "Close/Last" with "Close") for consistency and ease of reference during analysis.
  5. Integrating Datasets:
    • Linked the two datasets using the Ticker symbol, enabling combined analysis of stock trends and company attributes like sector and industry.
  6. Volatility Calculation:
    • Created a new column, Volatility, in the Historical Stock Data as the difference between High and Low prices, to quantify daily price fluctuations.
  7. Splitting Data into Time Periods:
    • Added a Year column derived from the Date field to facilitate grouping and trend analysis.
    • Divided the data into pre-2018 and post-2018 periods for comparative analysis of volatility.

Primary Analysis

Overview

In the primary analysis, we use statistical techniques and machine learning methods to address the research questions posed in the introduction. This phase builds upon the findings from the exploratory data analysis (EDA) and aims to uncover deeper insights into stock price trends, sector performance, and volatility patterns within the Nasdaq 100.

Key Objectives

  1. Analyze and quantify the relationship between stock price metrics.
  2. Evaluate changes in stock price volatility over time, particularly before and after 2018.
  3. Explore patterns and trends using machine learning techniques to support decision-making and trend prediction.

Methodology

The primary analysis was conducted through the following steps:

  1. Correlation Analysis:

    We began by analyzing the relationships between stock price metrics (Open, High, Low, Close) using a correlation matrix. This step provides insights into how these metrics move in relation to each other.

    • Findings: Strong positive correlations (values close to 1.0) were observed, indicating that these metrics are interdependent. For example, when the high price increases, the close price tends to increase as well.
    • Conclusion: The high degree of correlation suggests that trends in one metric can serve as proxies or indicators for others, simplifying future predictive modeling.
  2. Volatility Analysis:

    We calculated daily volatility as the difference between the high and low prices. To evaluate changes in volatility over time, we divided the dataset into two periods: pre-2018 and post-2018. A two-sample t-test was performed to assess whether the difference in volatility between these periods is statistically significant.

    • Null Hypothesis: There is no significant difference in stock price volatility before and after 2018.
    • Alternative Hypothesis: There is a significant difference in stock price volatility before and after 2018.
    • Results:
      • T-statistic: -31.32
      • P-value: 3.6e-182
    • Interpretation: The extremely low p-value (< 0.05) strongly rejects the null hypothesis, indicating that stock price volatility significantly increased post-2018.

    This analysis highlights the growing uncertainty and risk in the market during recent years, likely due to global economic disruptions such as the pandemic.

  3. Yearly Trends and Machine Learning-Based Insights:

    To further investigate trends in stock prices, we used linear regression to model yearly average close prices and predict future trends.

    • Linear regression was applied to the yearly average closing prices, showing a consistent upward trend over time.
    • The models slope and intercept indicate strong market growth, with periodic fluctuations accounted for in the residuals.
    • Future Application: The regression model can be used to predict potential average close prices for upcoming years, aiding in investment planning and strategy development.
  4. Sector-Specific Analysis:

    We analyzed sector-wise volatility and performance to identify which sectors contributed most to the changes in volatility and growth.

    • Technology and Consumer Discretionary sectors showed the highest average growth but also higher volatility, reflecting their dynamic and high-risk nature.
    • Healthcare exhibited steady growth with lower volatility, making it a more stable sector for investors.

# Calculate volatility as difference between High and Low prices
historical_data['Volatility'] = historical_data['High'] - historical_data['Low']

# Split data into pre 2018 and post 2018 periods
pre_2018_volatility = historical_data[historical_data['Year'] < 2018]['Volatility']
post_2018_volatility = historical_data[historical_data['Year'] >= 2018]['Volatility']

# Perform a t-test to check if volatility differs significantly between the two periods
t_stat, p_value = ttest_ind(pre_2018_volatility, post_2018_volatility)

# Display t-test results
print(f"T-statistic: {t_stat}, P-value: {p_value}")

T-statistic: -31.31587358156426, P-value: 3.600262292146294e-182

Null hypothesis: There is no significant difference in stock price volatility before and after 2018.

Alternative hypothesis: There is a significant difference in stock price volatility before and after 2018.

The t-test results in a t-statistic of -31.32 and a p-value of 3.6e-182. This extremely small p-value strongly rejects the null hypothesis, indicating that stock volatility has significantly increased after 2018.

Interpretation of the t-test result:

Since the p-value of 3.6e-182 is less than the significance value of 0.05, the result is statistically significant, meaning that the volatility of Nasdaq 100 before and after 2018 is significantly different.
